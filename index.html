Why This Study Matters

The question research forgot to ask.

Most research on formerly incarcerated students counts what it can measure: recidivism rates, risk scores, completion numbers, employment outcomes.

What it rarely asks is this: What does education actually mean to the person living it?

Seven formerly incarcerated learners and one researcher-participant answered that question. The findings changed how the question itself needs to be asked.

Positionality

From the Researcher's Own Voice.

The Researcher's Position

I am not an outsider looking in. I am a returning citizen who served a significant amount of time, came home, navigated the same institutions these participants describe, and now works inside higher education. I studied this experience as someone who has lived it, and I am one of the eight voices in this study.

Who Usually Tells This Story

Criminologists counting recidivism. Program evaluators measuring outcomes. Policy analysts assessing risk. Trained to study this population from a distance, and too often mistaking the record for the person behind it.

This study brings those two positions together in one person. The lived experience supplies insight no outside researcher can reach; the scholarly training holds that experience to the same rigor as every other account. That is not a conflict of interest. It is the methodology.

Problem and Purpose

Not subjects of inquiry. Knowledge holders.

The Problem

Formerly incarcerated learners have largely been positioned as subjects of inquiry, not as knowledge producers about their own educational lives.

The Gap

They have been treated as people to be studied and measured, rarely as people who hold real knowledge about their own educational lives. The expertise of the person who lived it goes unasked.

What This Study Set Out to Understand
How do formerly incarcerated students interpret college experiences in ways that shape resilience, identity reconstruction, and navigation of systemic barriers?

Methodology · Strand One

Listening for what lived experience feels like.

Hermeneutic Phenomenology
van Manen, 1997 & 2014

Seven in-depth interviews, each one transcribed, read, and re-read in cycles: as a whole story, then passage by passage, then line by line, then as a whole story again. Phenomenology does not ask what happened. It asks what it was like.

Earlier readings were revisited and revised as new understandings emerged. The depth of this study came from returning to each account again and again, not from collecting once and moving on.

Methodology · Strand Two

From the inside out.

Autoethnography
Chang, 2008 · Ellis et al., 2011

Most research on justice-impacted learners is conducted by people who have not lived the experience. Autoethnography disrupts that. It treats the researcher's own lived experience as legitimate data, held to the same critical analysis as every participant interview.

In plain terms: his story did not get a pass because it was his. It was coded, analyzed, and tested against seven other accounts with the same scrutiny. The lived experience supplies the data. The method supplies the discipline.

Finding · Theme Two
The Human Center of This Study

The Individual Who Showed Up.

Why it matters: it was never a program that changed a life. It was always a person.

Every participant in this study could name one individual: a mentor, an educator, an administrator, someone who saw them before they could fully see themselves. That person did not have a formal intervention title. They simply showed up with consistency, without conditions, at the moment it mattered most.

William named two people: an older man he met in prison who read everything he could get his hands on, and a case manager who sat with him after release to complete his financial aid application.

"That combination, this man planting something years before and this woman handing me a way in when I got out, that's what made it real."
William · Research Participant
Institutions create conditions. People create possibility. This finding asks every college to examine not just its programs, but the people inside them.

Finding · Theme Three
The Finding That Challenges Institutions

Disclosure as Paradox.

Why it matters: honesty about a record was both the price of entry and the thing that could get you turned away.

Being honest about a record opened some doors and closed others. Participants described disclosure not as a moment but as a recurring calculation: when to tell, who to tell, what it would cost, what it might unlock. This study found something deeper than individual choice. Disclosure functioned as a structural condition of access, shaped by institutional policies, campus culture, and the unspoken rules of who belongs in which rooms.

"I didn't have doors start opening for me until I started disclosing it. But it was kind of a special hell in and of itself to disclose all of this in order to get opportunities."
Maya · Research Participant
Telling the truth about your record was both a risk and a requirement. That is not a personal problem to manage. It is an institutional design problem to solve.

Finding · Theme Five
Where the Research Lands

Generative Commitment.

Why it matters: the point of finishing was never personal success. It was what you could do for the next person.

The endpoint was never personal success alone. Participants described education as meaningful precisely when it stopped being about them and started being about who they could help: mentoring others, giving back, opening a path for the people coming up behind them. This finding connects directly to Maruna's theory of desistance and McAdams' redemption narrative. The completion of a degree was not the finish line. It was the beginning of an obligation.

"The most important thing is that in my work, I support young students and my peers to realize their dreams."
Rob · Researcher-Participant (Dr. Robert L. Hudson)
Education became meaningful not when it was received, but when it was returned. That is what this research means by transformation.

The Argument

Beyond measurable success.

Every person in this study arrived carrying numbers: a GPA, a risk score, a record. Those numbers are real, and they matter. But each one measures something narrow, and misses the person standing behind it.

The Measure
What It Misses
The GPA
Records that you passed the course.
It cannot record what the course did inside you. Maya passed her classes and stopped believing she was, in her own words, an irredeemable soul.
The Risk Score
Judges you by what you did, not what you are doing now.
Lead with risk and you build barriers by default, not opportunities. Aaron once used drugs near the campus where he later took classes; today he is a peer recovery specialist who returns to support people still inside.
The Credential
Records that you finished.
It cannot record why finishing mattered. Malcolm earned his degree, but the point was never the diploma. It was his son, whose future he was investing in, and everyone he wanted to build a better path for.
This is what "beyond measurable success" means: the things we measure matter, but there is always more to a person than a GPA, a risk score, or a record can hold.
Measure only what is easy to count, and you build barriers around people instead of opportunities for them.

"Not everything that counts can be counted, and not everything that can be counted counts."

Research Limitations

What this study cannot claim.

Every study has boundaries. Naming them is not a weakness. It is the intellectual honesty that makes the findings trustworthy. Three of these point most clearly to where future research must go.

It went deep, not wide

Where research goes next

Eight people, not hundreds. This study can tell you what education meant to them. It cannot tell you how often the same thing happens across the wider population of justice-impacted learners.

Years had already passed

Where research goes next

Everyone had been home at least five years, most for ten or more. Meaning settles with time. For someone in the first hard months after release, when barriers are sharpest, the experience may look very different.

Not everyone was in the room

Where research goes next

Seven of eight participants were men. Only one woman, one juvenile experience, a limited range of racial and ethnic backgrounds. Whole populations of justice-impacted learners are not yet represented here.

The researcher was inside the story

An insider's view shapes interpretation. Reflexive journaling, member checks, and peer debriefing were used to make that influence visible and accountable, not to pretend it away.

One conversation, one method

These findings reflect what people said in a single interview. The same person, on a different day or through a different method, might tell it another way.

So Now What?

So now what?

If you have been incarcerated or justice-impacted and you are in a classroom now, or thinking about it: the doubt you feel in rooms that were not built for you is a predictable response, not a personal failing. You do not need a hidden talent waiting to be discovered. You are already becoming who you are by showing up, and what you have lived through is the foundation for what comes next. Your transformation is ongoing work, never a finish line, and its deepest meaning may be what it makes possible for the people who come after you.

What colleges and universities should do

Invest in people, not just programs

For every student in this study, one person made the difference: a mentor, an advisor, someone who showed up. Fund and support those relationships directly, instead of assuming a program will do it.

Make disclosure safe

Right now, telling the truth about a record can cost a student their place. Write policies so that being honest opens doors instead of closing them, and so no student has to manage that risk alone.

Measure more than completion

Track belonging, stability, identity, and whether students go on to help others, not just graduation and employment rates. Colleges resource what they measure, so the measures have to change.

Fix access at the front door

Admissions, financial aid, and background checks decide who gets in before a class is ever taken. Review those policies for the barriers they quietly create for justice-impacted applicants.

What I Learned

What this work taught me.

I am not alone.

I learned that lived experience carries more knowledge than we ever imagine, and that justice-impacted learners are holding insight no one has discovered yet. I learned that higher education is not a credential you collect. It is a way of living. And I learned that the things we do not wear on our sleeves, the parts shaped by what we survived, may be the strongest assets we have.

These are not just feelings. They are established theory: Mezirow's transformative learning, Maruna and McAdams' redemption narrative, Crenshaw's intersectionality. The participants confirmed what these frameworks predict, and pushed them further.

Higher education after incarceration is about showing up in rooms that were not designed for you, building a different narrative, and using what you learned to help the people coming up behind you.
